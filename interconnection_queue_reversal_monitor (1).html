Why This Leads
Every datacenter must secure grid access through an interconnection queue managed by the regional ISO — a process that takes 2–5 years. Queue entry is therefore the earliest observable signal of capex intent, historically leading visible capex adjustments by 12–18 months; given the unprecedented current backlog, the lag may extend to 18–30 months. Net MW additions are the most direct measure of whether developers are still committing to new capacity versus abandoning it.

Reversal confirmed — net MW turned decisively negative in March: At −696 MW net for the month, March is the largest negative net flow in the 24-month window. Combined with −252 (Jan '26) and −53 (Feb '26), the three-month net is −1,001 MW — the first sustained negative sequence on record. Index fell to … from a peak of ….
2
Additions momentum −17% on a 3-month basis — steepest decline since tracking began: The 3-month average of 5,149 MW/mo compares to a prior 3-month average of 6,240 MW/mo. Hyperscaler filings that sustained the queue boom through H1 2025 are not being replaced by equivalent new entrants. The addition pipeline is thinning as the queue/demand ratio (2.76x) signals that existing backlog already exceeds projected demand through 2030.
3
Withdrawal level near cycle highs despite a marginal MoM improvement: Monthly withdrawals edged down from 4,440 to 4,391 MW — a minor improvement, but the underlying level remains structurally elevated. Not all withdrawals signal demand destruction: some reflect projects repricing uneconomic upgrade costs or relocating to less congested ISOs. The risk is that the largest, most capital-intensive projects withdraw first, leaving a queue that looks healthier on average but has lost its most consequential capacity.
4
Wait times at 4.3 years — P90 at 7.0 years creates a structural mismatch with hyperscaler build schedules: Average wait has risen +1.6 years over the 24-month window. For hyperscalers on 18–24 month build timelines, 4+ year queues incentivise alternatives: behind-the-meter generation, bilateral PPAs, and relocation to less congested ISOs. These channels bypass the interconnection process entirely — meaning some MW leaving the queue does not translate into cancelled datacenter capacity.
5
FERC Order 2023 reforms provide no near-term relief — the wait-time floor is structural: The average wait has been at or above 4.0 years since mid-2025. FERC Order 2023 process improvements are in early implementation; LBNL estimates measurable impact on actual queue processing will not be visible before late 2027. Prior cycles (2018–19 solar/wind queue) showed a 15–25% reduction in construction starts with a 12–18 month lag — but the current queue is far larger, suggesting the transmission to reported capex could take 18–30 months.
